Introduction

Robot hardware design process

  1. Understand and examine the application, tasks, workspace and payload requirements.
  2. Determine the conceptual solution - the kinematic chain underlying the mechanical structure based on family of tasks and shape of workspace. (Robot type: serial, parallel or hybrid. Type, number and sequence of joints: revolute, prismatic, etc.)
  3. Determine the geometric dimensions of the various links defining the robotic architecture to satisfy workspace requirements.
  4. Determine the structural dimensioning of the various links and joints to meet (i) static load requirements, where load includes both forces and moments in the most demanding operation conditions, and (ii) dynamic load requirements, where loads are inertia effects of links and manipulated object.
  5. Select the actuators and their mechanical transmissions for the operating conditions.
  6. Design the mechanical structure including the actuator dynamics, to avoid/suppress vibrations.

If a system of links and joints has mobility M=0 or less, yet still moves, then it is called an overconstrained mechanism. The reason of over-constraint is the unique geometry of linkages in these mechanisms, which the mobility formula does not take into account.

Robot configurations and workspace

Some terminology

  1. Robot configurations: Combination and order of different kinds of joints that constitute the robot kinematic chain. Also referred to as the kinematic conceptual solution.
  2. Configuration space: A specification that expresses the position of all points of the robot. The n-dimensional space containing all possible configurations of the robot is called the configuration space (C-space) where n = dof.
  3. Workspace: The collection of points that can be reached by the robot’s end-effector. This depends on the robot architecture and the dimensions of the links and joints. It can be empirically found by moving the joints through all combinations of their range of motions.
  4. Task space: The space in which a robot’s task can be naturally expressed. This is independent of the degrees of freedom or the workspace of the robot and depends on the task itself.

Efficiency in attaining workspace

Longer links require more material to build (generally speaking). To help in optimizing this, we define length sum of a manipulator, L

L=∑i=1N(ai−1+Li)

Where a_(i-1) is the joint offset and L_i is the length of the link, i.

Structural length index, QL

QL=L/W3

Where W is the work volume of the manipulator

If QL is large, it means more material is needed to achieve the same work volume.

Grashof's rules

  • Base or frame: the fixed link, attached the frame of reference

  • Turning links: two, connected to the base by revolution joints

    • Crank, complete revolution relative to the base
    • Rocker, only oscillating

  • Coupler: jointed to both turning links, and has no direct connection to the base

s+l<p+q

s: the length of the shortest side

l: the length of the longest side

p, q: the length of the other two side

Grashof type 1 linkage (s+l < p+q)

Obey the inequality

Have two joints that perform complete rotations and two that oscillate between motion limits.

The two fully rotatable joints are those on the ends of the shortest link.

Subtypes

  1. If the shortest link is jointed to the base, the linkage is a crank-rocker. The joint between the shortest link and the base is fully rotatable, is a crank. The other fully rotatable joint connects that crank to the coupler.

  1. If the shortest link is the base, both joints at the base are fully rotatable, and both links connected to the base are cranks. The linkage is a double-crank, also known as a drag-link. It may be conveniently actuated at either of the base joints

  1. if the shortest link is the coupler, neither base joint is fully rotatable. The linkage is a type 1 double-rocker.

Grashof type 2 linkage (s+l>p+q)

Do not obey the inequality

Have no fully rotatable joints. All four joints then oscillate between motion limits.

Grashof neutral linkage(s+l=p+q)

This is called a transition linkage or Grashof neutral linkage. In this case, the linkage can assume a "flattened" configuration.

Basic Description

  1. A robot manipulator is composed of a set of links connected by joints

  2. Joints can be either

    1. revolute joint (a rotation by an angle about fixed axis)
    2. prismatic joint (a displacement along a single axis)
    3. more complicated joints (of 2 or 3 degrees of freedom) are represented as combinations of the simplest ones

  3. A robot manipulator with n joints will have (n + 1) links. Each joint connects two links

  4. We number joints from 1 to n, and links from 0 to n. So that joint i connects links (i − 1) and i

  5. The location of joint i is fixed with respect to the link (i − 1)

DH Convention

The key requirement of DH convention co-ordinate frames assignment is

  1. X-axis attached to ith link
  2. xi should be NORMAL (法线) to the Z-axis of ith and Z-axis of i−1th link

Assigning co-ordinate frames – x and z axes

Assigning z-axis

zi is aligned along the axis of the revolute joint.

In case of a prismatic joint, zi is aligned along the direction of translation of the joint.

Assigning x-axis

Case 1: z_i and z_(i-1) are not co-planar

x_i is unique normal to z_i and z_(i-1)

Case 2: z_i and z_(i-1) parallel

x_i is any normal to z_i and z_(i-1) (usually aligned/coincident with link 1)

Case 3: z_i and z_(i-1) intersect

x_i is normal to the plane of z_i and z(i-1) with the origin at the intersection of z_i and z(i-1)

Existence of solutions

Workspace: The collection of points that can be reached by the robot’s end-effector in atleast one orientation. Dexterous Workspace: The collection of points that can be reached by the robot’s end-effector in any given orientation

If the desired position and orientation of the wrist frame is in the workspace, then at least one solution exists.

Dexterous Workspace ⊆ Reachable Workspace

Multiplicity of solutions

Manipulators can often have multiple solutions for reaching the same Goal frame. You should choose one solution. Different criteria may be applied for the choice of solution. A simple criterion is the ‘closest’ solution (the once that can be attained by minimum change in joint angle. If links of different lengths are present in the manipulator, weights may be assigned to the joint angles so as to prioritize moving the smaller links over the larger ones
